The old observatory stood at the edge of the town, just beyond the last row of houses and the narrow road that led down to the river. Every evening, Mira climbed the hill with a notebook under her arm and a small lamp in her coat pocket. The building had not been used by the university for many years, but the brass telescope still turned smoothly, and the dome still opened with a low, patient sound.

On clear nights she recorded the weather, the color of the moon, and the movement of boats passing below. She was not looking for anything dramatic. She liked the quiet work of noticing small changes: a new crack in the stone steps, a fox crossing the garden, a fisherman tying a red ribbon to the pier before dawn. These details made the town feel less like a place she lived in and more like a book she was slowly learning to read.

One winter night, after a week of rain, the clouds broke apart and the sky became unusually sharp. Mira saw a faint light moving against the fixed pattern of stars. It was too steady to be a plane and too slow to be a meteor. She followed it for almost an hour, writing down each position with careful hands. By morning, she knew she had found something worth telling, but she also knew that discovery was only the beginning. Someone would need to check her notes, compare the old charts, and decide what the light truly was.
